Abstract

This paper explores the cultural continuity between Japanese girls’ culture and contemporary girls’ anime.
The study focuses on the transformation of symbolic figures, particularly the shift from the “princess” symbol to the “idol” symbol.

In modern Japanese girls’ culture, European imagery such as aristocracy, dresses, and royal settings often function as symbolic representations of an idealized world. These elements appear in works such as The Rose of Versailles. In school-based girls’ culture, known as S-culture, the symbolic focus shifts from distant princess figures to admired senior students.

Later, yuri culture further develops these relationships into narratives centered on emotional and romantic connections. Works such as Revolutionary Girl Utena and Maria-sama ga Miteru illustrate this transitional stage.

In contemporary girls’ anime, the symbolic structure transforms once again. The traditional princess figure evolves into the idol figure, representing participation, recognition, and self-expression. Series such as Pripara demonstrate this shift, where the ideal is no longer a distant aristocratic figure but an identity accessible to everyone.

Recent works may represent a fusion of these symbols, combining princess imagery with idol culture. This suggests a continuing transformation of symbolic structures within Japanese girls’ media.

This study proposes that girls’ anime should be understood not as isolated children’s entertainment but as part of a longer cultural lineage connecting modern girls’ literature, S-culture, yuri culture, and contemporary idol narratives.
